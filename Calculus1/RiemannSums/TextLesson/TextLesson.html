4.1 - Riemann Sums

If you have never used this symbol to sum things up, click here @@Link to summation notation in Algebra@@. Otherwise, keep reading.

Here is a little reminder of how a sum works Summation notation is very convenient way to write out a sum that follows a pattern, especially if it is an infinite sum and you don’t want to spend the rest of yours and your children’s lives writing out a sum. We will be doing infinite sums in the next section..

We want to find the the area under a curve. We do it by approximating the area with rectangles. This is what a Riemann Sum is. Since we will be using a bunch of rectangles for some of these, we represent the area using summation notation.

There are three common ways of drawing rectangles to approximate areas under curves: midpoint rectangles, Left endpoint rectangles, and right endpoint rectangles.

So, you take a curve like this one: I am a CURVE!

  1. Identify the starting and ending lines of your area.
  2. Divide it up into the number of sections you want to use. If you need rectangles, make four sections by drawing vertical lines equally spaced between your starting and ending lines.
  3. If you use left endpoint rectangles, go to the left side of each section. Follow the line up to where it touches the curve, and draw the line that goes on the top of the section (i.e. from left to right). This might make your rectangle above the curve or below the curve. After you do that to each rectangle, you find the area of each rectangle, and add them all together, and that gives you an approximation for the area under the curve. This is basic geometry and arithmetic once you figure out where your rectangles are.
