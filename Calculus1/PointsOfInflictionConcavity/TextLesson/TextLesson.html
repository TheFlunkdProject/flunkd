3.12 - Points of Inflection, Concavity

If you want to know about the concavity of a function (if it curves up or down), or about its points of inflection (where its concavity changes), you use the second derivative.

If a function is concave up, it is curving in an upward direction. A parabola like y=x^2 is curved upwards The second derivative is the rate of change of the rate of change, so it's like the rate of change of the slope. When a slope changes, the result is a curved graph., so we say that it is "concave up." y=-x^2 is concave down.

There is a little thing called the “second derivative test.” What we were using before to find local minima and maxima is called the "first derivative test."

You use the second derivative test by finding the critical numbers the normal way, and then finding out if the second derivative is positive or negative at each of those points. If the second derivative is positive, you have a minimum. If it's negative, you have a maximum. One way to remember the negativemaximum, and positiveminimum rule is to use these happy and sad faces:
