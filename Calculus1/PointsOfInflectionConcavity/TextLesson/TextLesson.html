Linear approximation is using a line to approximate a number. Let’s say we wanted to approximate with linear approximation.

First, you take it apart: What is the number, and what is the function?

x=2

Then you ask yourself, “What number close to 2 could I use for x that would be easy to figure out?” Well, 1 is close to 2, and 1=1, so let’s use that. We call that a.

a=1

Then we plug our things into this formula:

Linear approximation uses a tangent line at a point on a curve that is close to the point we are interested in. It traces the tangent line over to above or below where the actual point is, and it uses the y coordinate of the tangent line’s point as an approximation for the y coordinate of the actual point. Of course if the curve happens to curve a lot, the linear approximation won’t be that great. If you wanted to get more accurate, you could use a parabolic approximation, where not just the position and first derivative of the approximating curve are equal to the actual curve’s position and first derivative, but the second derivative is as well. You learn more about this idea in Calculus 2 with Taylor Polynomials. They are pretty great.
